## When voice goes global, operations get complicated

Most enterprise teams do not struggle with telephony because they lack features. They struggle because voice sits at the intersection of people, regulation, carriers, and day-to-day support. Add multiple geographies and the complexity multiplies.

We often meet organisations where each country has grown its own approach over time. Different carriers, different contracts, different numbering plans, and different processes for moves, adds, and changes. Reporting looks different in every region, and the service desk ends up firefighting issues it cannot see end-to-end.

That fragmentation tends to show up in predictable places. Acquisitions bring in new carrier estates that never fully get rationalised. A change to a call queue works in one region but not another because the underlying routing is different. Even basic questions like "how many active numbers do we have" or "which sites still rely on legacy kit" take weeks to answer.

Cloud telephony is usually part of the fix, but only if it is implemented with a clear operating model. Otherwise you just swap an on-prem estate for a cloud estate that is still fragmented.

## What we mean by cloud telephony

Cloud telephony is the PSTN (Public Switched Telephone Network) calling layer delivered as a managed cloud service. In practice, it connects your users and call flows to public voice networks, and it gives you a consistent way to manage numbers, routing, and policies across locations.

In a multinational environment, the value is not only technical. It is the ability to run voice with one set of controls, one support motion, and one view of what is live, even when the underlying carrier relationships and regulations differ by country.

For many enterprises, Microsoft Teams Phone becomes the user interface for calling. The architectural question is how you connect Teams to the PSTN in each country, and how you govern that connection at scale.

It is worth being specific about what "govern" means here. It includes how you request and assign numbers, how you control international dialling, how you handle emergency calling, and how you prove changes were made correctly when something is audited.

## How LoopUp approaches multinational rollout

At LoopUp, we focus on making global voice manageable for IT (information technology), not just functional for end users. That usually comes down to three things.

First, a consistent connection model to Microsoft Teams Phone. Depending on your requirements, that can be delivered through Operator Connect or Direct Routing. The point is not the label, it is standardising how sites get brought on, how changes are controlled, and how incidents are diagnosed.

Some organisations also evaluate Microsoft Calling Plans for specific countries or user groups. The right choice depends on coverage, control, commercial model, and how much consistency you need across regions.

Second, central governance with local fit. Multinational voice always has local constraints, numbering rules, emergency services, and in-country expectations from the business. A workable approach sets global policy for security, provisioning, and reporting, while still supporting the local requirements that keep the service compliant and usable.

Third, operational visibility. When something breaks, teams need to know where. Is it the user, the network, Microsoft Teams Phone, the routing, or the carrier? The faster you can pinpoint the boundary, the less time you spend escalating across suppliers.

## Trade-offs to plan for

Global standardisation is valuable, but it is never free.

You will need to decide how much variation you can tolerate by country. Some organisations insist on a single global design even when it adds cost or limits choice locally. Others allow controlled exceptions when the business case is clear.

You should also plan for coexistence during migration. It is rare to switch every location at once, so you need a design that supports legacy PBX (private branch exchange) connectivity, gradual porting, and phased adoption without turning support into a maze.

There are also country-level realities you cannot ignore. Number porting timelines vary, emergency services obligations differ, and some locations need in-country PSTN connectivity for regulatory or resilience reasons. A good design acknowledges those constraints early so they do not surface as last-minute blockers.

Finally, treat telephony as an operating service, not a rollout project. Numbers, users, call flows, and policies change constantly. The steady-state process matters more than the go-live date.
